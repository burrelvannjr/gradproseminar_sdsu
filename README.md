## Course Description

Graduate school is "professional" school. As a graduate student, you transition from being simply a "consumer" of knowledge to becoming an expert and "producer" of knowledge. Part of your training is to learn the practices of the fields of criminal justice and criminology.

This seminar is a space for dialogue and practice in building a professional identity — from developing research specializations and engaging relevant literatures, to honing academic writing skills and cultivating a public-facing presence through web profiles and CVs. Students will engage in discussions about the faculty in the School and gain training essential to program success, including time management, strategies for performing well in courses, seeking mentorship, and navigating graduate study.


## Course Learning Objectives

By the end of this course, students will be able to:

1. Explain the purpose of graduate study
2. Articulate the various research areas within the fields of criminal justice and criminology
3. Develop a strong research question and hone their writing skills
4. Design an academic plan that suits their current and future goals
5. Describe the research specializations of faculty
6. Engage faculty to meet mentorship goals
7. Practice the art of presentations

## Assignments

### "Preparing for the Course" Module (35 pts)
Completed during Week 1. Includes verifying the email policy, setting up Google Doc submissions, installing Zotero, and updating your course profile.

### Course Engagement (50 pts)
Attendance and active participation are critical to success in this seminar. Consistent presence enables meaningful dialogue about professional development and the graduate experience.

### Research Question Quiz (10 pts)
One quiz designed to help students develop a strong, focused research question related to their field of interest.

### Interests Presentation (5 pts)
A short presentation in which students introduce their research interests and personal background.

### RQ Presentation (5 pts)
A short presentation of the student's research question.

### CV Presentation (10 pts)
Students present a draft of their curriculum vitae for class feedback.

### Faculty Presentation (5 pts)
Students present on a faculty member in the School, covering their research specializations.

### Academic Timeline (5 pts)
Students develop a timeline mapping out their academic goals and milestones in the program.

### Draft Literature Review (15 pts)
An early draft literature review submitted to allow students to complete work incrementally, receive feedback, and acclimate to the process of academic writing and revision.

### Edited Literature Review (10 pts)
A revised and polished 1–2 page literature review submitted at the end of the semester, using ASA/APA citation format.

### Extra Credit

## Course Policies

### Late Work
Late work is generally not accepted. In extreme circumstances, late submissions may be considered with written documentation, but this is not guaranteed.

### Email
Include the course number and time in all emails. Emails are checked Monday–Friday, 9:00 AM–5:00 PM. Allow up to **72 hours** for a response. If you miss a class meeting, first check Canvas for materials, then reach out to classmates via Canvas before contacting the instructor.

### Academic Integrity
Cheating and plagiarism are strictly prohibited per SDSU Executive Order 1098. Violations may result in a failing grade and disciplinary review, up to and including suspension or expulsion.
